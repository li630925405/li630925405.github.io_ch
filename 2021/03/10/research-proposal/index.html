Virtual Placement of Objects in Acoustic Scenes

Motivation

As Augmented Reality experiences are growing in importance, it is increasingly of interest to develop advanced ways to insert “auditory objects” within mixed virtual-real scenes. Examples of auditory objects include musical instruments, humans speaking and so on. This approach has enormous potential to increase immersion in music consumption in films, games and streamed content. Because virtual rooms are multi-user environments, loudspeakers are typically used for sound reproduction instead of headphones.

Accurate localization and dispersion (sound propagation) are two essential aspects for true spatial sound to be delivered to the listener. The advantage of this over current approaches is that the virtual objects will have realistic dispersion characteristics and will interact acoustically as if they are really present in the physical space they are being rendered into. One exciting possibility is for new ways to enjoy live music concerts streamed to the home. There is potential to combine this technology with audio up-mixing, to enhance e.g. legacy stereo recordings for immersive 3D sound using Deep Learning based Source Separation.

Background

For the localization of virtual sound source, commonly known and used multi-channel loudspeaker reproduction methods are ambisonics, wave ﬁeld synthesis (WFS) and vector based amplitude panning (VBAP) [1]. In amplitude panning, the same sound signal is applied to two or more loudspeakers equidistant from a listener with appropriate amplitudes. The listener perceives an auditory object in a location that does not coincide with any of the physical sound sources, which is called a virtual source [2]. VBAP as well as ambisonics are readily available for use in common software environments for real-time processing, but they require that the listeners are restricted to a relatively small listening area surrounded by loudspeakers arranged in a circle or sphere. To solve this problem, distance-based amplitude panning (DBAP) is proposed to offer an alternative panning based spatialization method where no assumptions are made concerning the layout of the speaker array nor the position of the listener [3].

For sound propagation, algorithms can be divided into two major classes: wave and geometric. Wave-based techniques numerically solve the wave equation and can provide very accurate results but are computationally expensive [4], making them impractical for interactive applications. On the other hand, geometric sound propagation techniques are better suited for interactive applications and can generate plausible effects. These approaches simplify the computation of acoustic effects by assuming that the wavelength of sound is much smaller than the size of primitives in the scene. Image source methods , beam tracing [5] and frustum tracing [4] have been proposed for computing specular reﬂections, while Monte Carlo path tracing is commonly used for computing diffuse reﬂections [6]. The effect can also be implemented using parametric ﬁlter structures and the parameters for the ﬁlters are estimated using artiﬁcial neural networks [7].

Research problem

1. Sound propagation: the computation of impulse responses (IRs) that represent an acoustic space. (modeling how sound waves propagate through a medium. Eﬀects such as reﬂections, transmission, and diﬀraction are the important components.)

a) Indoor rendering

b) Outdoor rendering

Indoor rendering and outdoor rendering are two situations for sound propagation simulation and we can use these two scenarios to simulate concerts indoor or outdoors.

We should use efficient geometry methods to render sound propagation.

2. Virtual Sound Source Localization

We can use Amplitude Panning methods to insert “auditory objects” to virtual scenes.

3. Audio up-mixing using Deep Learning based Source Separation
